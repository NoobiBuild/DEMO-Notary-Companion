ğŸ” The Notarial Seal & Protocol

The Seal

Every notary must have an official seal bearing their name and the words "Notary Public, Republic of South Africa". The seal must be impressed on all notarial deeds. No seal = document is void.

âš ï¸
Exam trap: A notarial deed without the notary's seal is not merely irregular â€” it is null and void. This is a common MCQ distractor.

Protocol Book

Notaries must maintain a Protocol â€” a register of all notarial deeds executed. Original deeds remain in the protocol; parties receive certified copies (engrossments).

  • Protocol is numbered sequentially
  • Kept indefinitely â€” it is not your private property; it belongs to the state
  • On death/retirement, Protocol is handed to the Master of the High Court
  • Parties may request certified copies at any time
ğŸ¤ Duties of a Notary â€” The Core Obligations
Impartiality

A notary does not represent a party â€” they represent the law. You cannot notarise a deed where you have a personal interest, or where a close family member is a party.

Verification

You must verify the identity of all parties, ensure they understand what they are signing, and confirm they are acting voluntarily and with full mental capacity.

Confidentiality

Notarial instruments are public documents once registered â€” but the contents of advice and consultations remain privileged. Ethical rules of legal practice still apply.

ğŸš¨

The ANC is valid between the parties from date of execution. But between execution and registration, third parties are NOT bound. Once registered, registration is retrospective to date of marriage. Third parties are bound from registration date.

ğŸ’¡
An unregistered ANC is not void â€” it is enforceable inter partes. This is a favourite exam distinction.

An ANC may contain a forfeiture clause â€” providing that a spouse who causes dissolution of the marriage through misconduct forfeits the benefits of the ANC (e.g., forfeits the accrual). Courts have discretion to enforce or set aside such clauses.

ğŸ“Œ
Forfeiture clauses are valid but their enforcement is NOT automatic â€” the court decides based on fairness.

Long-term Leases
When a Lease Becomes More Than a Contract

A lease of land exceeding 10 years â€” or for a person's lifetime â€” enters a different legal universe. Notarial execution and Deeds registration transform it into a real right.

ğŸ¦‰
Nota Says

The key insight: a short lease (under 10 years) is purely contractual â€” it binds the parties but not third parties. A long lease (10+ years or for life), once notarially executed and registered, becomes a real right. This means: even if the landlord sells the land, the new owner is bound by the lease. Huur gaat voor koop â€” but only for registered long leases.

ğŸ“… The 10-Year Threshold â€” What Triggers Notarial Requirements
Leases Requiring Notarial Execution
  • Lease of land for a period exceeding 10 years
  • Lease of land for the lifetime of the lessee or lessor
  • Lease renewable at the option of the lessee such that the total period may exceed 10 years
  • Lease subject to a right of pre-emption / right to purchase
What Happens Without Registration?

An unregistered long lease is valid between the parties (landlord and tenant). But it does NOT bind a third party who purchases the land. The new owner can evict the tenant despite the lease. Registration = real right = "follows the land."

ğŸ“‹ Formal Requirements for a Long Lease
  1. 1Notarial execution. The lease must be prepared and executed before a Notary Public. Both parties sign in the presence of the notary and two witnesses.
  2. 2Registration at Deeds Office. The notarial lease must be lodged for registration. It is endorsed on (registered against) the title deed of the property.
  3. 3Consent of mortgagee. If the property is mortgaged, the bank/mortgage holder must consent to the registration of the lease â€” otherwise, the mortgagee's rights may be prejudiced.
  4. 4Description of property. The Erf/farm number, extent, and title deed reference must be precisely stated. A description that cannot be found in the Deeds Office will be rejected.
  5. 5Material terms. Rental amount, period (start and end dates or duration), renewal options, escalation clauses, and permitted use must all be clearly stated.
ğŸ’¼ Key Contents of a Long Lease Deed
PARTIES: Lessor & Lessee (IDs, capacity) PROPERTY: Full title reference, Erf/farm no. PERIOD: Commencement + duration or end date RENTAL: Amount, frequency, escalation % RENEWALS: Options & mechanism USE: Permitted purpose IMPROVEMENTS: Who pays; removal rights DOMICILE: Addresses for legal service
âš–ï¸ Rights of Parties â€” Long Lease

Lessee's Rights (once registered)

  • Real right to occupy and use the land
  • Right to sublease (unless prohibited by lease)
  • Protected against sale of the property â€” new owner must honour the lease
  • Can mortgage the leasehold right

Lessor's Rights

  • Rental payment enforceable as a real right condition
  • Right to cancel for breach (subject to notice)
  • Reversionary right at end of lease
ğŸ”„ Renewal Options & Exam Traps
ğŸš¨

ğŸš¨

ğŸ’¡

If the leased property is bonded, the mortgagee's written consent is an absolute requirement for registration of the long lease. Without it, the Deeds Office will reject the lodgement. This protects the bank's security â€” the lease could otherwise diminish the value of the property.

Servitudes
Rights That Run With the Land

Servitudes are real rights limiting the use of property for the benefit of another. Understand the taxonomy, the requirements, and the ways they are created and terminated.

ğŸ¦‰
Nota Says

The root word is servire â€” "to serve." A servitude is a burden on one property (or person) serving another. The classic mental model: your neighbour's land is burdened with your right of way across it. Their land serves yours. Master the praedial vs personal divide and you've cracked 80% of servitude questions.

ğŸ—‚ï¸ Classification of Servitudes
PRAEDIAL SERVITUDES
Attached to land â€” benefits a piece of land
The right benefits the dominant tenement (the property being served FOR)
The burden rests on the servient tenement (the property being burdened)
Runs with the land â€” transfers with ownership automatically
Examples: Right of way (via), right to draw water (aquaeductus), right to graze (pastus), right to light, right of support
Must be: praedio praedioque utilis â€” the servitude must benefit the dominant tenement, not just the owner personally
PERSONAL SERVITUDES
Attached to a person â€” benefits a specific individual
Benefits a specific person â€” not a property
Terminates on death of the beneficiary (or end of specified period)
NOT transferable or heritable
Usufruct: Right to use AND enjoy fruits of another's property
Usus: Right to use only (no right to take fruits for commercial purposes)
Habitatio: Right to dwell in a building
â•â– Positive vs Negative Servitudes
Positive Servitudes

Allow the holder to do something on the servient land (e.g., walk across it, draw water). The servient owner must tolerate the activity.

Negative Servitudes

Prevent the servient owner from doing something they would otherwise be entitled to (e.g., building above a certain height, blocking light). The servient owner must refrain from acting.

ğŸ›ï¸ Usufruct â€” The Most Important Personal Servitude

Rights of a Usufructuary

  • Use the property (occupy it, use it for its purpose)
  • Enjoy the fruits â€” natural fruits (crops, animals) and civil fruits (rental income)
  • Let the property to third parties
  • Mortgage the usufruct right (not the property itself)

Duties of a Usufructuary

  • Take a cautio usufructuaria (inventory + bond to the owner)
  • Maintain the property â€” keep it in good repair
  • Pay ordinary rates, taxes, and maintenance costs
  • Return the property in the same condition at end of usufruct

Termination of Usufruct

  • Death of the usufructuary
  • Expiry of the period for which it was granted
  • Merger â€” usufructuary becomes owner of the property
  • Renunciation by usufructuary
  • Destruction or loss of the thing
  • Prescription (non-use for 30 years)
ğŸ“Œ
A usufruct cannot be transferred to another person. The usufructuary may exercise the right through others (eg, lease), but the usufruct itself is personal and inalienable.
ğŸ“‹ Creating a Servitude â€” The Notarial Requirements
  1. 1Notarial deed. All servitudes created by agreement must be in a notarial deed signed by the parties before a Notary Public with two witnesses.
  2. 2Registration at Deeds Office. The servitude must be registered against BOTH the dominant and servient title deeds (for praedial) or against the property title (for personal).
  3. 3Clear description. The nature, extent, and location of the servitude must be clearly described. For a right of way: width, route, start/end points. Diagrams can be attached.
  4. 4Consent of bondholder. If the servient tenement is mortgaged, the mortgagee's written consent is required for registration.
ğŸ’¡
Other ways a servitude can arise (without notarial deed): Prescription (30 years uninterrupted use, openly, as of right), destination of paterfamilias (arising when subdivision of property occurs and the situation requires a servitude), testamentary disposition.
ğŸ”‘ Requirements for a Valid Praedial Servitude
1. Praedio Praedioque Utilis

The servitude must benefit the dominant tenement (the land) â€” not merely the owner personally. If neighbour A gets a right of way not because it benefits their land but because it's convenient for them personally, it may be a personal servitude or invalid praedial.

2. Tenements Must Be Neighbouring

The dominant and servient properties must be in reasonable proximity. They need not share a boundary, but a right of way over property in a different town for land in another province would be suspect.

3. Servitude Must Be Possible

The right must be physically and legally capable of being exercised. A servitude of obstruction (forcing the owner to do nothing) is valid. A servitude requiring active positive conduct (facere) from the owner is generally not allowed.

4. Civiliter Modo Exercise

The beneficiary must exercise the servitude in a civilised manner â€” causing the least possible inconvenience to the owner of the servient tenement. The servitude cannot be extended beyond its original scope.

ğŸï¸ Public Servitudes

Created by statute or government action (e.g., servitudes for roads, railways, power lines, municipal water pipes). They are not created by notarial deed but by legislation or expropriation. They benefit the public at large, not a specific person or piece of land.

Powers of Attorney
Authority to Act in Another's Name

When a notarial power of attorney is required, what it must contain, and when it expires.

ğŸ¦‰
Nota Says

A power of attorney (POA) authorises an agent to act on behalf of a principal. Not all POAs need a notary â€” but when the act being authorised requires notarial form (like executing an ANC, transferring property, or registering a bond), the POA authorising it must ALSO be notarial. Follow the chain of formality.

ğŸ“œ When is a NOTARIAL POA Required?

  • Signing an ANCAn agent signing an ANC on behalf of a party needs a notarial POA
  • POA executed abroadAny POA to be used in South Africa for registration purposes, if executed outside SA, must be notarially executed and apostilled
  • Mortgage bondsAgent signing a mortgage bond must have a notarial POA
  • Company resolutionsSome company resolutions authorising property transactions require notarial certification
âš¡ Key Rules for POAs

1. Capacity of the Principal

The principal must have legal capacity at the time the POA is signed AND at the time the agent exercises it. A POA signed by someone who later becomes mentally incapacitated is automatically revoked by operation of law.

2. Specific vs General

  • Specific POA: Authorises one specific act (e.g., "to sign transfer deed for Erf 123")
  • General POA: Broad authority for multiple acts â€” used with caution

3. Termination

  • Death of principal or agent
  • Mental incapacity of principal
  • Insolvency of principal
  • Revocation (must be communicated to agent)
  • Completion of the act authorised
ğŸ” Advanced POA Concepts

Ratification

If an agent acts without authority, the principal may ratify the act. Ratification must comply with the same formalities as the original act â€” so if the act required notarial execution, ratification must also be notarial.

Durable (Enduring) POA

At common law, mental incapacity automatically revokes a POA. A "durable" POA specifically states that it survives incapacity. However, for property transactions, the Master of the High Court may still need to be involved. Not commonly used in SA, but worth knowing.

Stipulatio Alteri

A contract for the benefit of a third party. A POA can be used to accept such a benefit on behalf of the third party. The acceptance must be notarial if the underlying contract required notarial form.

  3. 3If the foreign country is NOT a Hague Convention country: the POA must be authenticated by the SA Embassy/Consulate or DIRCO in SA
  4. 4The Deeds Office may require a sworn translation if the POA is not in an official SA language
Deeds Office Requirements

A foreign POA lodged at the Deeds Office must be accompanied by:

  • âœ… The apostille or authentication certificate
  • âœ… A certified sworn translation (if required)
  • âœ… A certificate by a local notary confirming the foreign notary's status (in some cases)

    ğŸ–Šï¸ Execution of a Notarial Deed â€” Step by Step
    1. 1Identification of parties. The notary must sight original identity documents (SA ID, passport). The notary records the ID numbers in the deed and satisfies themselves of each party's identity.
    2. 2Reading of the deed. The notary must read the deed aloud to the parties, OR confirm the parties have read it and understood it. The deed must be in a language the parties understand.
    3. 3Acknowledgement of understanding. The notary must confirm each party understands the nature and effect of the document and signs freely and voluntarily.
    4. 4Signing. Parties sign every page (or in the margin) and sign and initial the final page. All signatures must be made in the physical presence of the notary.
    5. 5Witness signatures. Two competent witnesses sign and their details (name, ID number, address) are recorded in the attestation clause.
    6. 6Notary signs and seals. The notary signs and impresses their official seal. The date and place of execution are recorded in the attestation clause.
    7. 7Protocol entry. The original is filed in the notary's protocol under a sequential number. The parties receive certified copies (engrossments).
    8. 8Registration (where required). The deed (or certified copy thereof) is lodged at the Deeds Office for registration within the prescribed time limits.
